# JPG vs PNG vs WebP: Which Format Should You Actually Use?

Three formats cover 95% of everyday image needs. Picking the wrong one costs you either quality or file size. Here's the practical decision guide.

## The one-line summary

- **JPG** — photos, when compatibility matters most.
- **PNG** — screenshots, text, logos, anything needing transparency.
- **WebP** — almost everything on the web, if your platform accepts it.

## JPG: the photo workhorse

JPEG uses lossy compression tuned for continuous tones — skies, skin, gradients. It makes photos small and opens literally everywhere.

Weaknesses: no transparency, and it smears hard edges. Text and UI screenshots saved as JPG get fuzzy halos around letters. Repeated re-saving degrades quality each time (generation loss), so keep an original and export copies.

**Use JPG for:** photos going to email, forms, old software, or print shops.

## PNG: pixel-perfect, at a price

PNG is lossless — every pixel preserved — and supports full transparency. That's why it's the default for screenshots and the right choice for logos and diagrams.

The price: photos saved as PNG are enormous, often 5–10× the JPG equivalent. A PNG photo attachment that won't fit an upload limit is one of the most common self-inflicted problems; converting [PNG to JPG](https://dengfan.top/png-to-jpg.html) usually solves it instantly.

**Use PNG for:** screenshots, text-heavy images, logos, transparency.

## WebP: smaller than both, if it's allowed

WebP does lossy and lossless, plus transparency, and typically beats JPG by 25–35% and PNG by more at similar quality. Every modern browser supports it, and it's the best choice for images on your own website ([why image weight affects SEO](https://dengfan.top/image-optimization-website-speed-seo.html)).

The catch is the long tail: some upload forms, older apps, and government portals reject it. When something refuses your .webp file, convert [WebP to JPG](https://dengfan.top/webp-to-jpg.html) or [WebP to PNG](https://dengfan.top/webp-to-png.html) and move on.

**Use WebP for:** your website, blog images, anywhere you control the platform.

Format choice gets you halfway; compression gets you the rest. If a platform enforces a hard limit, compress to the exact target — [100KB](https://dengfan.top/compress-100kb.html), [200KB](https://dengfan.top/compress-200kb.html), [500KB](https://dengfan.top/compress-500kb.html) — rather than eyeballing a quality slider.
